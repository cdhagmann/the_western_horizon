Core Philosophy

The Fundamental Principle

Content is generated responsively when players declare intent, not pre-generated in advance. Once established, it "always existed"‚Äîcommon knowledge available to all guild members.

Four Pillars of Western Horizon

1. Responsive Generation

Content is created when needed, not before. When a player posts "I want to investigate the ruins in the Thornwood," that's when you run Ex Umbra to build the dungeon. When they reach a new tier and need a larger settlement, that's when you run Ex Novo.

Why this works:

  • Eliminates wasted prep on content players never engage
  • Reduces DM burnout from pre-building an entire world
  • Ensures content is responsive to actual player interest
  • Allows the campaign to grow organically in unexpected directions
When Does "Responsive" Happen?

Not during the session. Responsive generation happens between sessions during prep. Player posts intent ‚Üí DM generates content before session ‚Üí Session runs with established content ‚Üí Discoveries logged after session.

2. Collaborative Authorship

The DM doesn't create the world alone. Players participate in worldbuilding through the generation systems themselves:

  • Session Zero: Everyone builds history (Microscope), sets tone (Palette), establishes the Guild
  • Settlement Sessions: Players help create towns using Ex Novo, detail buildings with Street Magic
  • Dungeon Prep: Rumors come from player speculation; Ex Umbra builds dungeons from those rumors
  • Discovery: When players find something, they help name it, describe it, define it

Why this works:

  • Players are invested in content they helped create
  • Reduces "guess what the DM wants" friction
  • Creates surprising, emergent stories no one person could plan
  • Distributes creative labor across the entire table
From Microscope's Principles

"Everyone is equal. We each have vast power to create and destroy. But once we make something, it belongs to all of us. No one owns anything."

This collaborative spirit extends beyond Session Zero into ongoing play. The world belongs to everyone at the table.

3. Canon Integration: "It Always Existed"

Once content is generated and played, it becomes permanent canon known to all guild members. The temple in the Thornwood? It always existed. The merchant prince who runs the harbor? Everyone knows about them.

How this works in practice:

  • Content is added to the Guild Wiki (Obsidian or similar)
  • Other players can reference it: "I heard there's a temple in the Thornwood..."
  • Future generation must not contradict established canon
  • New content builds on rather than replaces old content

Why this matters:

  • Creates a persistent shared world that grows session by session
  • Rewards long-term engagement‚Äîplayers see the consequences of their actions
  • Enables cross-party continuity: "The Azure Company cleared those ruins last month"
  • Removes "Schr√∂dinger's dungeon" problem‚Äîthings exist whether or not players visit them
What "Always Existed" Means

It doesn't mean the DM secretly planned everything in advance. It means once something appears in play, we treat it as if it was always part of the world. The generation method is invisible to players‚Äîthey just see a coherent, persistent world.

4. System Synthesis by Scale

The Western Horizon doesn't invent new worldbuilding procedures‚Äîit combines existing excellent games and shows when to use which one:

  • Each system is best-in-class for its scale
  • Clear handoff points between systems ("Ex Novo creates districts ‚Üí Street Magic details buildings within them")
  • Avoids reinventing the wheel‚Äîthese games have been playtested extensively
  • Modular‚Äîuse the systems you like, adapt or skip the ones you don't

How This Differs from Standard West Marches

Traditional West Marches assumes the DM pre-generates the entire world before play begins. The Western Horizon keeps the player-driven, open-table structure of West Marches but adds:

  • Responsive content generation instead of upfront world creation
  • Collaborative worldbuilding instead of DM-only world design
  • System guidance for which procedures to use when
  • Integration with modern tools (Obsidian wikis, Claude AI processing)
This Reduces DM Burden

West Marches campaigns are notorious for DM burnout. By generating content responsively and collaboratively, The Western Horizon makes West Marches sustainable for long-term play without requiring heroic prep efforts.

Guiding Principles from Source Games

These principles from Microscope inform how we use all the systems:

  • Everyone is equal. One person might facilitate, but they don't have more control than others during collaborative creation.
  • Don't contradict what's already established. New content builds on the foundation‚Äîit doesn't erase it.
  • Don't coach or suggest on someone else's turn. Let each person contribute their own ideas so we can be surprised.
  • Paint a clear picture everyone can visualize. Vague content is hard to build on‚Äîbe specific and evocative.
  • Players are more important than the game. If something makes someone uncomfortable, address it immediately. Safety tools override all other rules.

What This Framework Provides

The Western Horizon gives you:

  • Procedures for when and how to use each worldbuilding system
  • Workflows from player intent ‚Üí content generation ‚Üí session play ‚Üí wiki documentation
  • Integration points showing how systems hand off to each other
  • Oracle tables unified from all sources for quick reference
  • Templates for wiki pages, session prep, and quick reference
  • Philosophy tying it all together into a coherent approach

It does not replace the source games‚Äîyou'll need those for complete procedures, tables, and examples. Think of this as the conductor's score showing when each instrument comes in, not the sheet music for each instrument.
